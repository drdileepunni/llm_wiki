# Case Summary: Acute Decompensated Heart Failure — Conservative Diuresis with Cardiorenal Caution

**Source type:** Clinical case summary
**Setting:** Tertiary cardiac centre inpatient admission
**Patient:** Patient B — late-60s, female, known HFrEF (EF 28%)

---

## Presentation

Patient B presented with 7-day history of worsening dyspnoea and lower limb oedema. Weight was 6 kg above dry weight. Oxygen saturation 91% on room air. BNP on admission was 3,100 pg/mL. Creatinine was 162 µmol/L against a baseline of 115 µmol/L — a more significant acute-on-chronic kidney injury than Patient A. eGFR was estimated at 31 mL/min/1.73m².

---

## Management

### Diuresis
The treating team elected for a **conservative, stepped diuresis strategy** given the degree of renal impairment on presentation. Initial dose was furosemide 80 mg IV BD (bolus, not infusion). The rationale was that high-dose loop diuretic infusions in the context of significant CKD carry unacceptable risk of further AKI, potentially precipitating dialysis-dependent renal failure.

Urine output targets were set at >100 mL/hour (less aggressive than standard targets). Daily electrolyte and creatinine monitoring was performed. Net fluid removal of 2.5 L was achieved by Day 2 — slower than aggressive strategies, but with preservation of renal function.

**Outcome of diuresis:** Creatinine rose only to 174 µmol/L before plateauing and beginning to fall by Day 4. The team concluded that **conservative diuresis preserves renal function** in high-risk CKD patients and that forcing rapid decongestion risks permanent renal injury that outweighs the benefit of faster fluid removal. Worsening renal function during diuresis in this context represents true cardiorenal syndrome and warrants dose adjustment, not continuation of aggressive diuresis.

### Tolvaptan
**Tolvaptan 15 mg orally daily was added on Day 3** when urine output was deemed insufficient despite adequate loop diuretic dosing. The aquaretic mechanism (free water excretion without electrolyte loss) allowed additional fluid removal without further nephrotoxic loop diuretic escalation. Sodium remained stable. The team concluded that **tolvaptan is a valuable adjunct in patients with CKD and inadequate response to loop diuretics**, enabling decongestion while limiting renal risk.

### Neurohormonal Blockade
The patient's home sacubitril-valsartan was **held on admission** due to haemodynamic instability (SBP 85 mmHg on arrival) and significant AKI. The team's view was that continuing ARNi during active ADHF with AKI risks precipitating dangerous hypotension and worsening renal perfusion. **Sacubitril-valsartan should be withheld until the patient is euvolaemic, haemodynamically stable, and renal function is recovering.** It was reintroduced at Day 7 at a reduced dose.

### Vasopressors / Inotropes
Not required, though considered briefly given initial hypotension.

---

## Outcome

- Discharged Day 11 (longer than Patient A)
- Creatinine at discharge: 130 µmol/L (above baseline, but improved)
- BNP at discharge: 780 mg/mL (higher than Patient A at discharge)
- No re-admission at 30 days; however creatinine remained elevated at 30-day follow-up

---

## Key Clinical Conclusions

1. **In patients with significant CKD (eGFR <35), conservative diuresis is preferred** to avoid precipitating irreversible AKI. Worsening renal function during diuresis in this population is a signal to reduce, not increase, diuretic intensity.
2. **Tolvaptan is a clinically valuable aquaretic adjunct** in patients with CKD and suboptimal loop diuretic response. It facilitates decongestion without electrolyte disturbance or additional renal tubular toxicity.
3. **Sacubitril-valsartan should be withheld during ADHF** when there is haemodynamic compromise or significant AKI, and reintroduced cautiously after clinical stabilisation.
4. The primary treatment target in high-risk cardiorenal patients is **preservation of renal function alongside decongestion** — not decongestion at any cost.
