# Case Summary: Acute Decompensated Heart Failure — Aggressive Diuresis Strategy

**Source type:** Clinical case summary
**Setting:** Tertiary cardiac centre inpatient admission
**Patient:** Patient A — mid-60s, male, known HFrEF (EF 30%)

---

## Presentation

Patient A presented with a 5-day history of progressive dyspnoea, orthopnoea, and bilateral leg oedema. He was found to be 8 kg above his dry weight. Oxygen saturation was 88% on room air. Chest X-ray confirmed pulmonary oedema with bilateral pleural effusions. BNP on admission was 2,840 pg/mL. Creatinine was 138 µmol/L (baseline 110 µmol/L), representing mild acute-on-chronic renal impairment.

---

## Management

### Diuresis
The treating team elected for **aggressive IV diuresis** with furosemide 120 mg IV bolus followed by a continuous infusion at 20 mg/hour. The rationale was that achieving rapid decongestion was the primary goal, and that a transient worsening of renal function (WRF) was acceptable in the context of significant fluid overload.

Urine output targets were set at >200 mL/hour. Daily weights and strict fluid balance were monitored. Net fluid removal of 4.5 L was achieved by Day 2.

**Outcome of diuresis:** Creatinine rose transiently to 178 µmol/L by Day 3 before returning toward baseline by Day 6. Despite this, congestion resolved rapidly. BNP fell to 620 pg/mL by Day 5. The team concluded that **aggressive diuresis was safe and effective** and that the transient WRF did not represent true cardiorenal syndrome but rather pre-renal physiology that self-resolved with decongestion.

### Tolvaptan
**Tolvaptan was not used.** The team's view was that tolvaptan (V2 receptor antagonist / aquaretic) adds cost without clinically meaningful benefit over loop diuretics when aggressive dosing is employed. Sodium levels were normal throughout; there was no indication for aquaresis.

### Neurohormonal Blockade
The patient's home sacubitril-valsartan (97/103 mg BD) was **continued throughout the admission** without interruption. SBP remained >90 mmHg. The team's view was that continuing ARNi during ADHF reduces adverse remodelling and that the evidence does not support routine cessation during decompensation. eGFR was monitored closely and remained acceptable.

### Vasopressors / Inotropes
Not required. Patient remained warm and well-perfused throughout.

---

## Outcome

- Discharged Day 8 at dry weight
- Creatinine at discharge: 118 µmol/L (near baseline)
- BNP at discharge: 540 pg/mL
- No re-admission at 30 days

---

## Key Clinical Conclusions

1. **Aggressive IV diuresis (high-dose loop diuretic infusion) is the first-line strategy for ADHF with significant fluid overload.** Transient WRF should not prompt diuretic dose reduction if the patient remains haemodynamically stable and congested.
2. **Tolvaptan adds no meaningful benefit when aggressive loop diuretic therapy is available** and should not be routinely used in eunatraemic ADHF.
3. **Continuing sacubitril-valsartan during ADHF is safe and preferable** to cessation in haemodynamically stable patients.
4. Decongestion, measured by BNP trend and daily weight, is the primary treatment target. Transient creatinine rise should not divert attention from this goal.
