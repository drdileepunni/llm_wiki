# Patient B 2026 — ADHF Conservative Diuresis Case

**Citation:** Clinical case summary. Patient B — late-60s, female. Tertiary cardiac centre. 2026.

**Source type:** Clinical case report

---

## Abstract

This case report documents the management of acute decompensated heart failure in a patient with significant chronic kidney disease (eGFR 31 mL/min/1.73m²) and reduced ejection fraction (28%). The treating team employed a conservative diuresis strategy prioritizing renal preservation over rapid decongestion, using lower-dose loop diuretics, strict monitoring, and the addition of tolvaptan as an aquaretic adjunct. Sacubitril-valsartan was withheld during acute decompensation due to hypotension and acute kidney injury, then cautiously reintroduced after stabilization. The case demonstrates successful management of high-risk cardiorenal patients where preservation of renal function is weighted equally with decongestion.

---

## Key Findings

- **Conservative diuresis in advanced CKD (eGFR <35) preserves renal function** — furosemide 80 mg IV BD rather than high-dose infusion, urine output target >100 mL/hour rather than aggressive targets
- **Tolvaptan 15 mg daily as aquaretic adjunct** enabled additional decongestion without electrolyte disturbance or further nephrotoxic loop diuretic escalation
- **Sacubitril-valsartan should be withheld during ADHF** when haemodynamic compromise (SBP 85 mmHg on presentation) or significant AKI present; reintroduced Day 7 at reduced dose
- **Worsening renal function during diuresis in CKD patients signals need to reduce diuretic intensity**, not escalate — creatinine rose from 162 to 174 µmol/L before plateauing and improving
- **Slower decongestion is acceptable when it preserves renal function** — net 2.5 L removal by Day 2, discharge Day 11 with creatinine 130 µmol/L and no 30-day readmission

## Clinical Details

### Presentation
- Late-60s female with known [[HFrEF]] (EF 28%)
- 7-day history worsening dyspnoea and lower limb oedema
- Weight 6 kg above dry weight
- SpO₂ 91% on room air
- BNP 3,100 pg/mL
- Creatinine 162 µmol/L (baseline 115 µmol/L) — acute-on-chronic kidney injury
- eGFR 31 mL/min/1.73m²
- SBP 85 mmHg on arrival

### Management

**Diuresis:**
- [[Furosemide]] 80 mg IV BD (bolus, not infusion)
- Urine output target >100 mL/hour (less aggressive than standard)
- Net fluid removal 2.5 L by Day 2
- Creatinine peaked at 174 µmol/L Day 2-3, then began falling Day 4
- Daily electrolyte and creatinine monitoring

**Aquaretic adjunct:**
- [[Tolvaptan]] 15 mg PO daily added Day 3 for insufficient urine output despite adequate loop diuretic dosing
- Enabled additional fluid removal without electrolyte loss or further loop diuretic escalation
- Sodium remained stable

**Neurohormonal blockade:**
- Home [[sacubitril-valsartan]] held on admission due to hypotension and AKI
- Reintroduced Day 7 at reduced dose after euvolaemia and haemodynamic stabilization

**Vasopressors/inotropes:**
- Not required (considered briefly given initial hypotension)

### Outcome
- Discharge Day 11
- Creatinine at discharge: 130 µmol/L (above baseline 115, but improved from peak 174)
- BNP at discharge: 780 pg/mL
- No readmission at 30 days
- Creatinine remained elevated at 30-day follow-up

## Clinical Conclusions from Source

1. In patients with significant CKD (eGFR <35), conservative diuresis is preferred to avoid precipitating irreversible AKI
2. Worsening renal function during diuresis in CKD population is a signal to reduce, not increase, diuretic intensity
3. Tolvaptan is clinically valuable aquaretic adjunct in patients with CKD and suboptimal loop diuretic response
4. Sacubitril-valsartan should be withheld during ADHF when haemodynamic compromise or significant AKI present
5. Primary treatment target in high-risk cardiorenal patients is preservation of renal function alongside decongestion — not decongestion at any cost
