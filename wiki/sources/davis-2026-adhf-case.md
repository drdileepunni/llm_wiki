# Davis 2026 ADHF Case Report

## Citation
Patient: John M. Davis (initials J.M.D., MRN 004-87-2231)
Clinical Setting: General Cardiology Service, attending Dr. Sarah Nguyen, MD
Admission: April 1-6, 2026
Document completed: April 6, 2026

## Abstract
A 75-year-old male with ischemic cardiomyopathy and HFrEF (EF 25-30%) presented with acute decompensated heart failure precipitated by dietary indiscretion and medication non-adherence. He demonstrated severe volume overload (12 lb weight gain, bilateral lower extremity edema to thighs, pulmonary edema) and atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response. Treatment included IV furosemide infusion, rate control optimization, and careful GDMT advancement despite concurrent cardiorenal syndrome (creatinine rose from 1.8 to 2.4 mg/dL during diuresis). By discharge, net fluid removal exceeded 7 kg, BNP fell from 3,840 to 980 pg/mL, and new therapies (sacubitril/valsartan, dapagliflozin) were successfully initiated. Case demonstrates practical management of ADHF with attention to decongestion goals, cardiorenal balance, and aggressive GDMT optimization during hospitalization.

## Key Findings

**Clinical Presentation:**
- NYHA Class IV symptoms on admission (orthopnea requiring 4 pillows, PND, dyspnea at rest)
- Massive volume overload: 12 lb weight gain over 10 days, 3+ pitting edema to thighs, JVD to jaw, bilateral pulmonary crackles
- Atrial fibrillation with RVR (HR 118 bpm)
- BNP 3,840 pg/mL on admission
- Cardiorenal syndrome: creatinine 2.1 mg/dL on admission (baseline 1.8), peaked at 2.4 during diuresis

**Precipitating Factors:**
- Dietary indiscretion (high sodium intake during family gatherings)
- Medication non-adherence (missed furosemide doses)
- Possible CPAP non-compliance (obstructive sleep apnea)

**Therapeutic Interventions:**
- IV furosemide: initial 80 mg bolus, then continuous infusion 10 mg/hr, titrated down to 7.5 mg/hr for cardiorenal concerns, transitioned to IV bolus 80 mg BID by day 3, then oral 80 mg BID
- Rate control: metoprolol succinate uptitrated from 50 to 100 mg daily
- Net fluid removal: 7.4 kg over 5 days (admission weight 104.2 kg → discharge 96.8 kg)
- GDMT optimization during admission:
  - Sacubitril/valsartan 24/26 mg BID initiated (ACE-i held ≥36 hours prior)
  - Dapagliflozin 10 mg daily added (SGLT2 inhibitor)
  - Spironolactone 25 mg daily continued
  - Furosemide dose doubled from home regimen

**Clinical Course Markers:**
- BNP trajectory: 3,840 → 1,640 (day 3) → 980 pg/mL (day 5)
- Creatinine trajectory: 2.1 → 2.4 (day 2) → 2.2 (day 3) → 2.0 mg/dL (day 5, near baseline)
- Oxygen requirement: 4L NC on admission → room air by day 3
- Functional status: bed-bound with dyspnea → ambulating independently by day 5

**Echo Findings:**
- January 2026 (baseline): EF 25-30%, global hypokinesis, moderate MR, elevated RVSP 42 mmHg
- April 4, 2026 (during admission): EF 20-25% (slightly reduced, attributed to acute myocardial stress), moderate-to-severe functional MR, no new wall motion abnormalities

**Discharge Planning:**
- Home health nursing 3x/week for 2 weeks
- Remote telemonitoring enrolled
- Structured follow-up: HF clinic in 7 days, cardiology in 14 days, nephrology in 9 days
- Patient education emphasized: daily weights, fluid/sodium restriction, medication adherence, CPAP compliance
- Advanced heart failure team referral placed

## Open Questions

1. **Optimal timing for GDMT optimization:** This case successfully initiated sacubitril/valsartan and dapagliflozin during the admission itself. What percentage of ADHF admissions achieve similar GDMT advancement before discharge, and does earlier initiation improve outcomes?

2. **Cardiorenal balance during diuresis:** Creatinine rose from 2.1 to 2.4 mg/dL during aggressive diuresis but returned near baseline by discharge. What thresholds should trigger diuretic dose reduction versus acceptance of transient worsening renal function in pursuit of adequate decongestion?

3. **Role of CPAP non-compliance:** Patient had documented obstructive sleep apnea with CPAP non-compliance. How much did OSA contribute to the decompensation, and what interventions improve CPAP adherence in heart failure populations?

4. **Echo EF decline during acute decompensation:** EF dropped from 25-30% (January) to 20-25% (April admission), attributed to "acute myocardial stress." Does this represent reversible myocardial stunning, or does it predict further deterioration? What is the expected trajectory with optimal GDMT?

5. **Dietary indiscretion as precipitant:** Patient admitted to high sodium intake during family gatherings. What behavioral interventions or support systems most effectively prevent recurrent dietary-related decompensations in elderly HF patients?

## Clinical Significance

This case exemplifies contemporary inpatient heart failure management with several notable features:

1. **Aggressive decongestion with cardiorenal monitoring:** The team pursued substantial fluid removal (>7 kg) despite transient creatinine elevation, consistent with emerging evidence that adequate decongestion outweighs modest worsening renal function.

2. **GDMT optimization during hospitalization:** Rather than deferring ARNI and SGLT2i initiation to outpatient follow-up, these were started prior to discharge, potentially improving long-term adherence and outcomes.

3. **Structured transition of care:** Home health, telemonitoring, and closely-spaced follow-up appointments represent evidence-based strategies to reduce 30-day readmission.

4. **Documentation of precipitants:** Clear identification of dietary indiscretion and medication non-adherence as triggers enables targeted interventions.

The case also highlights persistent challenges: CPAP non-compliance, polypharmacy burden (9 discharge medications), and need for advanced heart failure evaluation given progressive disease despite optimal medical therapy.
