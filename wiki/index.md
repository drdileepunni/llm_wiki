### Urinary Tract Conditions
- [[uncomplicated-urinary-tract-infection]] — Bacterial infection of the bladder in healthy patients without structural abnormalities or comorbidities; also known as cystitis or lower tract UTI
- [[cystitis]] — Inflammation of the bladder, commonly caused by bacterial infection. Also known as [[uncomplicated-urinary-tract-infection]] or lower tract UTI.
- [[pyelonephritis]] — Upper tract UTI that can result from untreated lower UTIs, potentially leading to nephron damage and complications like hypertension.
- [[complicated-urinary-tract-infection]] — UTIs in patients with structural abnormalities, comorbidities (e.g., diabetes, immunocompromised state, recent urologic surgery, pregnancy), or in uncircumcised males.
- [[recurrent-urinary-tract-infection]] — Repeated UTIs (recurrence within 12 months occurs in ~50% of women after initial infection).
- [[asymptomatic-bacteriuria]] — Presence of bacteria in urine (>10^5 CFU/mL) without urinary symptoms; does not constitute a UTI unless in specific populations.
- [[bacteriuria]] — Presence of bacteria in urine. Does not constitute a UTI without symptoms.
- [[pyuria]] — Presence of white blood cells in urine. Does not constitute a UTI without symptoms; may indicate inflammation from other causes.
- [[hematuria]] — Blood in urine; valuable finding in UTIs as bacterial infection of bladder transitional cells often causes bleeding.
- [[nephron]] — Delicate renal structures susceptible to damage from [[pyelonephritis]].

### UTI Causative Organisms
- [[Escherichia coli]] — Causes majority of uncomplicated UTIs; gram-negative rod; can express adhesins for urothelial attachment
- [[Klebsiella]] — Second most common cause of UTIs; gram-negative rod; urea-splitting organism that raises urinary pH
- [[Proteus]] — Gram-negative rod; urea-splitting organism associated with alkaline urine and struvite stones
- [[Enterobacter]] — Gram-negative organism causing UTIs
- [[Enterococcus]] — Gram-positive organism; does not convert nitrates to nitrites; typically associated with complicated UTIs
- [[Pseudomonas]] — Gram-negative organism; does not convert nitrates to nitrites; associated with complicated UTIs
- [[Acinetobacter]] — Gram-negative organism; does not convert nitrates to nitrites; associated with complicated UTIs
- [[Staphylococcus saprophyticus]] — Gram-positive coccus; susceptible to pivmecillinam; causes ~5-10% of uncomplicated UTIs in young women
- [[Ureaplasma urealyticum]] — Urea-splitting organism that raises urinary pH

### UTI Antibiotics
- [[Nitrofurantoin]] — First-line agent for uncomplicated UTIs; bacteriostatic; 5-7 day course; poor tissue penetration (lower UTI only); contraindicated in GFR <45 mL/min; also used for long-term prophylaxis at low dose (50 mg QHS)
- [[Sulfamethoxazole-Trimethoprim]] — First-line agent; effective as 3-day mini-dose therapy; avoid if local resistance >20% or sulfa allergy; high resistance rates in some regions; alternative for prophylaxis
- [[Trimethoprim]] — Alternative to sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim in sulfa-allergic patients; can be used for prophylaxis
- [[Fosfomycin]] — FDA-approved single-dose therapy for uncomplicated UTIs; maintains therapeutic urinary concentrations for 2-4 days; effective against resistant organisms; may be used for prophylaxis in selected cases
- [[First-generation cephalosporins]] — Effective for 3-day mini-dose therapy; use limited due to resistance concerns
- [[Fluoroquinolones]] — High tissue penetration, preferred for pyelonephritis/prostatitis; high resistance rates; not preferred for uncomplicated UTIs; includes norfloxacin (designed for lower UTIs)
- [[Norfloxacin]] — Fluoroquinolone specifically designed for lower UTIs; poor renal tissue penetration (avoid in pyelonephritis); may be used for prophylaxis in selected cases
- [[Pivmecillinam]] — β-lactam antibiotic; FDA-approved April 2024 for uncomplicated female UTIs; prodrug of mecillinam; 185 mg TID x 3-7 days (US dosing) or 400 mg TID (European dosing); effective against E. coli, Proteus mirabilis, and S. saprophyticus; resistance low (1-6%); ~$100/course; inadequate for pyelonephritis; long-term prophylaxis use less preferred but effective against ESBL-producing and multidrug-resistant strains; generally safe in first trimester of pregnancy; risk of carnitine depletion with long-term use requiring patient monitoring for symptoms (muscle aches, fatigue, hyperammonemia, weakness, cardiomyopathy, confusion)
- [[Phenazopyridine]] — Adjunctive agent for symptomatic relief in UTIs; used for several days alongside antibiotics

### Adjunctive & Preventive Agents
- [[Cranberry]] — Juice, pills, or extract; shows 30-40% reduction in some UTI studies but significantly less effective than low-dose antibiotic prophylaxis; mechanism unclear
- [[D-mannose]] — Investigated for UTI prophylaxis with some evidence of benefit but insufficient data for formal recommendation
- [[Methenamine]] — Converted to formaldehyde in bladder when urinary pH <5.5; requires vitamin C for urine acidification; conflicting data on benefit in recurrent UTI prophylaxis; alternative to antibiotics in selected patients; contraindicated in GFR <10 mL/min
- [[Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)]] — Used to acidify urine in conjunction with methenamine or standalone; supports acidic urine environment for UTI prevention
- [[Lactobacillus rhamnosus]] — Probiotic organism; may support healthy vaginal microbiota; studied in combination with cranberry and vitamin C for recurrent UTI management

### Urinalysis & Diagnostics
- [[urinalysis]] — Laboratory analysis of urine to aid in diagnosis of disease
- [[uti-diagnosis-dipstick]] — Dipstick testing for UTI diagnosis: nitrites (>90% specificity), leukocyte esterase (sensitivity 62-98%, specificity 55-96%), blood, pH; PPV 85%, NPV 92% for nitrites + esterase combination
- [[uti-diagnosis-microscopy]] — Microscopic urinalysis findings: bacteria on Gram stain, WBCs >10 per high-power field indicate UTI in symptomatic patients; no bacteria should be visible in uninfected urine
- [[uti-diagnosis-urine-culture]] — Urine culture confirms UTI; current threshold ≥1000 CFU/mL in symptomatic patients (older threshold was ≥100,000 CFU/mL); ~20-40% of women with UTIs have ≤10,000 CFU/mL; recommended in all high-risk populations and treatment failures
- [[urine-specimen-collection]] — Proper collection technique essential for accurate results; midstream clean-catch preferred; epithelial cells indicate contamination; refrigerate if not sent immediately

### UTI Pathophysiology
- [[uti-pathophysiology-bacterial-adhesion]] — Bacteria express adhesins (e.g., fimbriae) allowing attachment to uroepithelial surface
- [[uti-pathophysiology-host-defense]] — Urine antimicrobial properties: pH <5, high urea, hyperosmolality, organic acids, proteins, nitrites; urothelial layer produces antimicrobial peptides and cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, IL-8); lactobacilli and acidic pH prevent uropathogens in premenopausal women; antibiotic use disrupts this protection
- [[uti-pathophysiology-mucosal-barrier]] — Bladder mucus layer serves as mechanical barrier; defect/injury predisposes to recurrent infections; superficial urothelial layer may shed when heavily infected, reducing bacterial load
- [[uti-pathophysiology-urinary-factors]] — High urine flow and frequent urination reduce UTI risk; glycosuria increases risk in diabetics; urea-splitting organisms raise pH and may form struvite stones

### UTI Diagnosis & Clinical Assessment
- [[uti-diagnosis]] — Multi-modal diagnosis based on clinical history, urinalysis, and urine culture; cannot rely on symptoms, dipstick, or culture alone
- [[uti-clinical-presentation]] — Dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency, suprapubic discomfort, hesitancy, bladder spasms, hematuria; absence of fever/chills/flank pain distinguishes from pyelonephritis; atypical presentation in older/neurologically impaired patients
- [[uti-special-populations]] — Older/frail patients: changes in mental status more reliable than dysuria; spinal cord injury: autonomic dysreflexia, spasticity changes, fever; catheterized patients: pyuria/bacteriuria common but does not indicate UTI without systemic symptoms
- [[symptomatic-vs-asymptomatic-bacteriuria]] — Distinction crucial for UTI diagnosis; bacteriuria or pyuria without symptoms is not a UTI and typically requires no treatment except in pregnant women, immunosuppressed patients, transplant recipients, preoperative patients, or post-urologic surgery

### UTI Management & Treatment
- [[uti-management]] — Aims to relieve symptoms and prevent spread to kidneys or progression to [[pyelonephritis]]; most uncomplicated UTIs resolve spontaneously (approximately 20% in healthy nonpregnant women) but patients seek therapy for symptom relief
- [[uti-treatment-duration-dosing]] — Standard treatment 3-14 days; 5-7 days most common; mini-dose therapy (3 days) shows excellent cure rates; men require ≥7 days; pivmecillinam 185 mg TID (US) or 400 mg TID (Europe) x 3-7 days; fosfomycin single dose; nitrofurantoin 5-7 days
- [[uti-treatment-first-line-selection]] — Nitrofurantoin preferred initial agent; sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim if local resistance <20%; fosfomycin if high resistance; pivmecillinam for resistant organisms; fluoroquinolones reserved for pyelonephritis/prostatitis; avoid if resistance >50%
- [[uti-treatment-antibiotic-resistance]] — E. coli resistance varies by region; if resistance to a particular agent exceeds 50%, select alternative; pivmecillinam resistance remains low (1-6%) despite 30+ years European use; rising resistance concerns with fluoroquinolones
- [[uti-treatment-failure]] — Occurs in 10-18% of patients with uncomplicated UTIs; causes include bacterial resistance, patient non-compliance, renal failure, reduced drug absorption, use of sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim, undiagnosed urological issues (incomplete bladder emptying, urolithiasis, prostatitis, pyelonephritis); risk factors include advanced age, chronic diarrhea, diabetes, foreign bodies in urinary tract, male gender, renal failure
- [[uti-prophylaxis]] — Low-dose long-term prophylaxis used for recurrent UTIs; typically lasts 6-12 months though may be extended to 2 years; [[nitrofurantoin]] 50 mg QHS preferred for long-term prophylaxis; [[sulfamethoxazole-trimethoprim]] or [[trimethoprim]] alone are alternatives; [[norfloxacin]] and [[fosfomycin]] may be used in selected cases; [[pivmecillinam]] considered when other agents cannot be used due to resistance but not typically preferred for long-term use; reduces recurrence risk
- [[uti-prophylaxis-carnitine-depletion]] — Long-term [[pivmecillinam]] therapy can lead to carnitine depletion; patients require monitoring for muscle aches, fatigue, hyperammonemia, weakness, cardiomyopathy, and confusion

### Recurrent UTI Management
- [[recurrent-uti-management]] — Optimizes personal hygiene, vitamin C as urinary acidifier, precautions after sexual contact, prophylactic antibiotics or antiseptics; see companion resource for detailed management
- [[uti-adjunctive-therapies]] — [[Cranberry]] (juice, pills, extract) shows 30-40% reduction in some studies but significantly less effective than low-dose antibiotic therapy; [[D-mannose]] has some evidence of benefit but insufficient data for formal recommendation; estrogen vaginal cream (applied twice weekly) beneficial for postmenopausal women with atrophic vaginitis; increased fluid intake helpful especially in women with low urinary volumes
- [[uti-prophylaxis-methenamine]] — [[Methenamine]] converted to formaldehyde in bladder when urinary pH <5.5; [[vitamin C]] used to acidify urine; shows some benefit in recurrent UTI prophylaxis but conflicting data; may be useful alternative to antibiotics in selected patients; contraindicated in severe renal failure (GFR <10 mL/min); failure of methenamine, D-mannose, and cranberry prophylaxis typically indicates need for low-dose long-term antibiotic therapy

### Physiology & Risk Factors
- [[uti-risk-factors]] — Female anatomy (shorter urethra), sexual intercourse, catheter use, diabetes, immunosuppression, urinary obstruction, incomplete bladder emptying, diaphragm/spermicide use, poor hygiene, kidney transplant, antibiotic use, neurogenic bladder, cystocele, dehydration, diarrhea, first UTI before age 15, frequent pelvic exams, IBS, menopause, maternal UTI history, multiple partners
- [[urinary-tract-abnormalities]] — Structural issues that differentiate complicated from [[uncomplicated-urinary-tract-infection]]; imaging (cystoscopy) generally not needed for uncomplicated cases but may be useful for relapsing infections to rule out stones and anatomical defects

### Prognosis & Complications
- [[uti-prognosis]] — Most women with UTI experience excellent outcome with symptoms resolving within 2-4 days of antibiotic treatment; nearly 30% experience recurrence within 6 months; mortality from uncomplicated UTI close to zero; morbidity more common in older/debilitated patients with significant comorbidities or renal calculi
- [[uti-poor-outcome-factors]] — Advanced age, bladder stones, chemotherapy, chronic diarrhea, diabetes (particularly if poorly controlled), incontinence, immobility, morbid obesity, nephrolithiasis, neuropathy or spinal cord injury, pelvic organ prolapse, poor overall health, history of overactive bladder, malignancy, prior radiation therapy, renal failure, sickle cell anemia, urethral catheterization
- [[uti-complications]] — Chronic prostatitis, emphysematous pyelonephritis and cystitis, focal renal nephronia, hypertension, incontinence, persistent lower urinary tract symptoms, progression to complicated UTIs, prostatic abscess, pyelonephritis, renal abscess, renal failure, staghorn urinary calculi; morbidity significant with distressing symptoms and high management costs; missed work/school common; hospital admission sometimes required

### UTI Prevention & Patient Education
- [[uti-prevention-patient-education]] — Key preventative strategies for women at higher risk: urinate after sexual intercourse to reduce bacterial presence (increases 10-fold after sexual activity); wipe front to back after urination to prevent contamination from rectal flora; maintain high-volume vigorous urine flow to flush bacteria; shower instead of baths to minimize bacterial exposure; use gentle fragrance-free liquid soap, baby soap, or baby shampoo (liquid soaps less likely to harbor bacteria than bar soap); apply soap with freshly cleaned soft cotton or microfiber washcloth; clean vaginal area first to prevent contamination
- [[uti-treatment-compliance]] — Patients should be advised to take medication exactly as prescribed and complete full course of antibiotics even if symptoms improve; prophylactic antibiotics should only be taken if specifically prescribed due to risk of increased bacterial resistance; comprehensive patient education crucial to preventing recurrences
- [[uti-clinical-management-barriers]] — Healthcare team understanding important for optimization: disagreement with guideline recommendations, drug-related issues (unavailability, insurance coverage, high costs), increased mid-level practitioner utilization not familiar with guidelines, limited guideline applicability to specific patients, limited knowledge of new agents like pivmecillinam, organizational issues (emergency/urgent care less likely to order cultures), patient allergies to first-line agents, patient compliance issues, perception of guideline inapplicability, incorrect assumption urine cultures never needed for uncomplicated UTIs, unavailability of testing materials (microscopes, dipsticks, culture supplies)

### UTI Differential Diagnosis
- [[uti-differential-diagnosis]] — Conditions to consider when evaluating dysuria and/or urinary frequency: [[bladder-stones]], [[complicated-urinary-tract-infection]], [[herpes-simplex-infection]], [[medication-adverse-effects]], [[overactive-bladder]], [[pelvic-inflammatory-disease]], [[prostatitis]], [[pyelonephritis]], [[recurrent-urinary-tract-infection]], [[relapsing-urinary-tract-infection]], [[renal-infarction]], [[renal-stones]], [[sexually-transmitted-infections]], [[urethritis]], [[vaginitis]]

### Spontaneous Resolution & Natural History
- [[uti-spontaneous-resolution]] — Even without treatment, uncomplicated UTIs spontaneously resolve in approximately 20% of healthy, nonpregnant women; resolution more likely with increased hydration; risk of developing acute [[pyelonephritis]] from untreated uncomplicated UTI is minimal in healthy women

### Healthcare Team Coordination
- [[uti-interprofessional-management]] — Optimal UTI management requires collaboration between physicians, mid-level practitioners, nurses, and pharmacists; barriers include disagreement with guidelines, drug unavailability, unfamiliarity with new agents like [[pivmecillinam]], organizational factors, and patient compliance issues; nurses play critical role in patient education and tracking progress; pharmacists ensure appropriate coverage, dosing, and duration; infectious disease consultation recommended for complex cases
