# Acute Decompensated Heart Failure (ADHF)

## Definition
Acute decompensated heart failure (ADHF) refers to the sudden or gradual worsening of chronic heart failure signs and symptoms, requiring urgent medical intervention, typically hospitalization. It represents a clinical syndrome of fluid overload and/or inadequate tissue perfusion.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms
- Dyspnea (at rest or with minimal exertion)
- Orthopnea (inability to lie flat)
- Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND)
- Fatigue, weakness
- Reduced exercise tolerance
- Abdominal bloating, nausea (from hepatic congestion)

### Physical Examination Findings
- Elevated jugular venous pressure (JVD)
- Pulmonary crackles/rales
- S3 gallop (ventricular gallop)
- Peripheral edema (typically bilateral, pitting)
- Hepatomegaly, hepatojugular reflux
- Cool extremities (in low-output states)
- Tachycardia, tachypnea
- Hypoxemia

## Common Precipitants

Identifying precipitating factors is crucial for preventing recurrence:

1. **Medication non-adherence** — especially diuretics
2. **Dietary indiscretion** — excessive sodium or fluid intake
3. **Acute coronary syndrome** — new ischemia or infarction
4. **Arrhythmias** — atrial fibrillation with RVR, ventricular arrhythmias
5. **Uncontrolled hypertension**
6. **Infections** — pneumonia, UTI, sepsis
7. **Renal dysfunction** — acute kidney injury
8. **Anemia**
9. **Thyroid disorders**
10. **Medications** — NSAIDs, corticosteroids, calcium channel blockers, antiarrhythmics
11. **Pulmonary embolism**
12. **Sleep apnea** — particularly if CPAP non-compliant

## Diagnostic Evaluation

### Laboratory Tests
- **BNP or NT-proBNP** — elevated in volume overload
- **Troponin** — to rule out acute coronary syndrome
- **Renal function** — creatinine, BUN, eGFR
- **Electrolytes** — sodium, potassium (hyponatremia common)
- **CBC** — assess for anemia
- **Liver function tests** — hepatic congestion
- **Thyroid function** — rule out thyroid disease

### Imaging
- **Chest X-ray** — cardiomegaly, pulmonary edema, pleural effusions, cephalization
- **Echocardiography** — assess EF, wall motion, valvular function, diastolic parameters
- **EKG** — assess rhythm, ischemia, prior MI

## Management Strategy

### Acute Phase (Inpatient)

**Primary Goals:**
1. Relieve symptoms (decongestion)
2. Optimize volume status
3. Maintain end-organ perfusion
4. Identify and treat precipitants

**Pharmacologic Interventions:**
- **IV diuretics** — first-line for volume overload (furosemide most common)
  - Can use bolus dosing or continuous infusion
  - Dose typically 1-2.5x home oral dose as IV equivalent
- **Vasodilators** — if elevated blood pressure (nitroglycerin, nitroprusside)
- **Inotropes** — if low cardiac output/hypoperfusion (dobutamine, milrinone) — use cautiously
- **Rate control** — if atrial fibrillation/flutter (beta-blockers, digoxin, amiodarone)
- **Oxygen** — to maintain SpO₂ >90-92%
- **Non-invasive ventilation** — CPAP/BiPAP if severe respiratory distress

**Monitoring:**
- Strict intake/output measurement
- Daily weights
- Continuous telemetry (arrhythmia monitoring)
- Serial renal function and electrolytes
- Serial BNP if available

**Supportive Care:**
- Fluid restriction (typically 1.5-2L/day)
- Sodium restriction (<2g/day)
- Consider nephrology consultation if cardiorenal syndrome

### Transition of Care

**Before Discharge:**
- Optimize guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT)
- Patient education (daily weights, dietary restrictions, medication adherence)
- Arrange close outpatient follow-up (ideally within 7 days)
- Consider home health nursing
- Consider remote monitoring/telemonitoring

## Evidence from Case Reports

### Davis 2026 Case ([[davis-2026-adhf-case]])

A 75-year-old with ischemic cardiomyopathy and HFrEF (EF 25-30%) presented with classic ADHF precipitated by dietary indiscretion and medication non-adherence:

**Presentation:**
- 12 lb weight gain over 10 days
- NYHA Class IV symptoms (severe orthopnea, PND, dyspnea at rest)
- BNP 3,840 pg/mL
- 3+ pitting edema to thighs, JVD to jaw, bilateral pulmonary crackles
- Concurrent AFib with RVR (HR 118 bpm)
- Cardiorenal syndrome (creatinine 2.1 mg/dL, up from baseline 1.8)

**Treatment Course:**
- IV furosemide continuous infusion (10 mg/hr, later reduced to 7.5 mg/hr for renal concerns)
- Net fluid removal: 7.4 kg over 5 days
- BNP declined: 3,840 → 980 pg/mL by discharge
- Creatinine trajectory: 2.1 → 2.4 (day 2) → 2.0 mg/dL (discharge, near baseline)
- GDMT optimization achieved during admission:
  - Sacubitril/valsartan 24/26 mg BID initiated
  - Dapagliflozin 10 mg daily added
  - Furosemide dose doubled
  - Metoprolol uptitrated

**Key Lessons:**
1. Adequate decongestion (>7 kg fluid removal) achieved despite transient creatinine elevation
2. GDMT advancement successfully completed during hospitalization rather than deferred to outpatient
3. Structured discharge planning (home health, telemonitoring, close follow-up) implemented to reduce readmission risk

## Prognosis

- ADHF hospitalizations carry significant morbidity and mortality
- 30-day readmission rates: 20-25%
- In-hospital mortality: 2-5%
- 1-year mortality post-hospitalization: 20-30%
- Recurrent admissions predict worse long-term outcomes
- Identifying and addressing precipitants reduces readmission risk
