# Loop Diuretic Nephrotoxicity

**Concept:** Acute kidney injury induced or worsened by loop diuretic therapy, particularly at high doses or in patients with pre-existing chronic kidney disease.

**Mechanism:** Multifactorial
- **Volume depletion** — excessive diuresis reduces renal perfusion
- **Activation of renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system** — vasoconstriction of afferent arteriole
- **Electrolyte disturbances** — hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia contribute to tubular injury
- **Direct tubulotoxicity** — possible but less well-established
- **Reduced prostaglandin synthesis** — in context of concurrent NSAID use

---

## Clinical Context

### Risk Factors

- Advanced [[chronic kidney disease]] (eGFR <35 mL/min/1.73m²)
- Pre-existing acute-on-chronic kidney injury
- Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, low cardiac output)
- High-dose loop diuretic therapy (especially continuous infusions)
- Concurrent nephrotoxic agents (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast)
- [[Cardiorenal syndrome]]

### Clinical Presentation

- Rising creatinine during diuresis
- Falling eGFR
- Oliguria despite adequate diuretic dosing
- Electrolyte derangements (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, metabolic alkalosis)

## Evidence from Sources

### Patient B 2026 — ADHF Conservative Diuresis Case

**Clinical scenario:**
- Patient with eGFR 31 mL/min/1.73m² presenting with [[acute decompensated heart failure]]
- Creatinine 162 µmol/L on presentation (baseline 115 µmol/L)
- Significant acute-on-chronic kidney injury

**Treatment team's assessment of nephrotoxicity risk:**

> "High-dose loop diuretic infusions in the context of significant CKD carry unacceptable risk of further AKI, potentially precipitating dialysis-dependent renal failure."

**Management to minimize nephrotoxicity:**

1. **Conservative [[furosemide]] dosing:** 80 mg IV BD (bolus) rather than high-dose continuous infusion
2. **Lower urine output targets:** >100 mL/hour (less aggressive than standard)
3. **Close monitoring:** Daily creatinine and electrolytes
4. **Response to rising creatinine:** When creatinine rose from 162 → 174 µmol/L, dose was **not escalated**; instead strategy was maintained and [[tolvaptan]] added as aquaretic adjunct

**Outcome:**
- Creatinine peaked at 174 µmol/L then improved to 130 µmol/L at discharge
- **Avoided dialysis-dependent renal failure**
- Successful decongestion achieved (BNP 3,100 → 780 pg/mL)

**Key clinical principle:**

> "Forcing rapid decongestion risks permanent renal injury that outweighs the benefit of faster fluid removal."

> "Worsening renal function during diuresis in this context represents true cardiorenal syndrome and warrants dose adjustment, not continuation of aggressive diuresis."

Source: [[Patient B 2026 — ADHF Conservative Diuresis Case]]

## Prevention Strategies

### In High-Risk Patients (eGFR <35, significant AKI)

1. **Use conservative dosing strategy** — lower doses, bolus rather than infusion
2. **Set less aggressive decongestion targets** — accept slower fluid removal
3. **Add aquaretic adjuncts** ([[tolvaptan]]) rather than escalating loop diuretic dose
4. **Monitor closely** — daily creatinine, electrolytes, urine output
5. **Reduce dose if creatinine rising** — interpret as sign of nephrotoxicity, not "diuretic resistance"
6. **Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins** — NSAIDs, contrast, aminoglycosides if possible
7. **Maintain haemodynamic stability** — avoid excessive hypotension

---

## Trade-offs

**Conservative approach:**
- **Benefit:** Preserves renal function, avoids dialysis-dependent renal failure
- **Cost:** Slower decongestion, longer hospital stay, prolonged symptoms

**Aggressive approach:**
- **Benefit:** Rapid decongestion, shorter hospital stay, faster symptom relief
- **Cost:** Higher risk of permanent renal injury in vulnerable patients

**Clinical judgment required:** In patients with advanced CKD or significant AKI, the risk of permanent renal injury typically outweighs the benefit of rapid decongestion.
