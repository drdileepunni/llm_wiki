# Neurohormonal Blockade in ADHF

**Concept:** The use (or withholding) of renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) inhibitors and neprilysin inhibitors during acute decompensated heart failure.

**Agents include:**
- ACE inhibitors (e.g. enalapril, lisinopril)
- Angiotensin receptor blockers / ARBs (e.g. valsartan, losartan)
- Angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitors / ARNIs (e.g. [[sacubitril-valsartan]])
- Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists / MRAs (e.g. spironolactone, eplerenone)

**Chronic HFrEF management:** Neurohormonal blockade is cornerstone therapy — ARNI, beta-blocker, MRA combination reduces mortality and hospitalization.

**Acute decompensation:** Controversy exists regarding whether to continue, reduce, or withhold these agents during [[acute decompensated heart failure]].

---

## Rationale for Withholding During ADHF

### Haemodynamic Concerns

- **Hypotension risk** — RAAS inhibitors reduce systemic vascular resistance; in setting of low cardiac output and systemic hypoperfusion, may precipitate dangerous hypotension
- **Reduced renal perfusion** — in context of low cardiac output, blocking compensatory RAAS activation may worsen renal perfusion and precipitate [[acute kidney injury]]
- **Haemodynamic instability** — patients with cardiogenic shock or borderline perfusion may not tolerate the vasodilatory effects

### Renal Concerns

- **Acute kidney injury** — RAAS inhibitors reduce efferent arteriolar tone, decreasing glomerular filtration pressure; in patients with significant AKI, this can worsen renal function
- **Hyperkalemia** — particularly with MRAs in context of reduced renal clearance

## Evidence from Sources

### Patient B 2026 — ADHF Conservative Diuresis Case

**Clinical scenario:**
- Patient on home [[sacubitril-valsartan]] (ARNI)
- Presented with acute decompensated heart failure
- **SBP 85 mmHg on arrival** (haemodynamic instability)
- **Creatinine 162 µmol/L** (baseline 115 µmol/L) — significant acute-on-chronic kidney injury
- eGFR 31 mL/min/1.73m²

**Management decision:** Sacubitril-valsartan **held on admission**

**Treating team's rationale:**

> "Continuing ARNi during active ADHF with AKI risks precipitating dangerous hypotension and worsening renal perfusion."

> "Sacubitril-valsartan should be withheld until the patient is euvolaemic, haemodynamically stable, and renal function is recovering."

**Re-introduction:** Day 7 at reduced dose

**Criteria for re-introduction:**
- Euvolaemia achieved (net 2.5 L removed by Day 2, continued diuresis)
- Haemodynamic stabilization (SBP recovered from 85 mmHg)
- Renal function recovering (creatinine improving from peak 174 µmol/L toward discharge value 130 µmol/L)

**Outcome:**
- Safe re-introduction without hypotension or further renal deterioration
- Discharge Day 11 with sacubitril-valsartan restarted at reduced dose

## Clinical Decision Framework

### When to Withhold Neurohormonal Blockade in ADHF

**Strong indications:**
- SBP <90 mmHg (haemodynamic instability)
- Significant acute kidney injury (creatinine rise >50% from baseline, or eGFR <30 with rising creatinine)
- Cardiogenic shock or hypoperfusion
- Severe hyperkalemia (K+ >5.5 mmol/L)

**Relative indications:**
- SBP 90-100 mmHg with symptomatic hypotension
- Moderate AKI (creatinine rise 30-50% from baseline)
- Need for vasopressor or inotropic support

### When to Continue Neurohormonal Blockade in ADHF

**Possible in:**
- Haemodynamically stable (SBP >100 mmHg, adequate perfusion)
- No or minimal AKI
- No hyperkalemia
- Close monitoring available

**Rationale for continuation:**
- Avoids neurohormonal activation rebound
- Maintains proven mortality benefit
- Avoids need for careful re-titration

---

## Re-introduction Strategy

**Timing:**
- After euvolaemia achieved (clinical decongestion, stable or improving weight)
- After haemodynamic stabilization (SBP consistently >95-100 mmHg)
- After renal function stabilizing or improving (creatinine plateaued or falling)

**Dosing:**
- Re-introduce at **reduced dose** (e.g. 50% of home dose)
- Uptitrate gradually over days to weeks as outpatient
- Monitor BP, creatinine, potassium closely

---

## Contradictory Views

Some guidelines and clinicians advocate for **continuation** of neurohormonal blockade during ADHF unless clear contraindications (severe hypotension, cardiogenic shock), arguing:
- Stopping agents risks neurohormonal rebound and worsening decompensation
- Proven mortality benefit should not be interrupted
- Temporary creatinine rise may be acceptable

**This source represents conservative approach** prioritizing haemodynamic and renal safety during acute phase.
